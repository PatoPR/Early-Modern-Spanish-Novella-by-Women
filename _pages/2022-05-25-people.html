Meet our team of dedicated researchers passionate about exploring Spanish women writers' works. Our team includes experts in Spanish literature, gender studies, and cultural history who bring a wealth of knowledge and experience to our research efforts. We are committed to uncovering the unique voices and perspectives of Spanish women writers throughout history and to sharing their important contributions with the world. Get to know our team members below and discover their individual areas of expertise and research interests.

 

Elizabeth S. Lagresa-González obtained her Ph.D. in Romance Languages and Literatures from Harvard University and is an Assistant Professor at the University of British Columbia, Vancouver. She specializes in early modern Spanish literature and culture, which she addresses at the intersection of gender, cross-cultural, visual and material studies. She has published multiple peer-reviewed articles, a critical edition and English translation of Bernat Metge’s Lo Somni / The Dream, as well as a forthcoming co-edited volume on “Literature and Economy in Spain and Beyond” from the Revista Canadiense de Estudios Hispánicos. Her monograph, titled “The Business of Romance,” examines the transculturation of objects and female subjects across national and disciplinary borders through the lens of new economic and material criticism.
Email: elagresa@mail.ubc.ca
Profile: https://fhis.ubc.ca/profile/elizabeth-lagresa/

Jennifer Nagtegaal is a Ph.D. candidate in Hispanic Studies at UBC. Jennifer’s M.A. and doctoral research, funded by the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada (SSHRC), develops in the areas of animation studies and comics studies, respectively, and a number of recent projects at the intersection of both. This is the case of her monograph, Politically Animated: Non-Fiction Animation from the Hispanic World, forthcoming with the University of Toronto Press (August 2023). Politically Animated offers the first book-length study on animated non-fiction from the Spanish-speaking world.

Jennifer worked as a Graduate Research Assistant on this project from 2022-3. Jennifer’s early contributions focused on preparing a comprehensive bibliography for the women writers under study (specifically, primary and secondary sources for Leonor de Meneses and Ana Francisca Abarca de Bolea), as well as the annotation of these two individual bibliographies. A second phase of her work saw the translation of these annotated bibliographies into English.

Email: jennifer.nagtegaal@ubc.ca
Profile: https://fhis.ubc.ca/profile/jennifer-nagtegaal/

María Fernanda Guzmán was born in Orizaba, Veracruz, Mexico, and has loved to write since she was little. She studied literature at the Universidad de las Américas Puebla and had two academic exchanges at City Beijing University (China) and the University of Salamanca (Spain). She just finished her M.A. in Hispanic Studies at the University of British Columbia. She has worked as an editor of books published by the Management of the Historic Center of Puebla. She was also a high school teacher at Prepa Anahuac Puebla. Her tastes are diverse: she likes to stream video games, read historical novels and write fantasy. She recently published her first book Arcángel: los niños elegidos.

Email: maryferg198@gmail.com

Mirella Reichenbach Livotti is a Master´s student in the Hispanic Studies Program at the University of British Columbia (UBC). She also completed her undergraduate studies with a Double Major in Sociology and Latin American Studies from the same university. Her research interest is centred primarily on the intersection of gender and race in contemporary Latin American literature. In addition, she is interested in the representations of gender-based violence and its connection to work being done by feminist social movements and intellectuals. For this project, Re-imagining the Hispanic Novella, Mirella is responsible for creating a critical annotated bibliography of the author María Egual and her novel El esclavo de su dama.  

Email: milivoti@student.ubc.ca

 

Pamela Zamora is a doctoral candidate in Hispanic Studies at the University of British Columbia. Her dissertation revolves around how 20th and 21st Central American and Mexican fiction contests humanitarian crises such feminicido, indigenous genocide and forced migration in the neoliberal epoch. At the intersection of sound studies, feminist theory and migration studies, her work amines to trace the literary representation of those crises. Pamela collected primary and critical sources to develop an exhaustive bibliography database on female writers from the Golden Age.

Profile:  https://fhis.ubc.ca/profile/pamela-zamora-quesada/

Sarah Revilla is a Ph.D. student in Hispanic Studies at UBC. Her research lies at the intersection of gender and cultural studies, particularly interested in gendered violence, affect, and artivism (art+activism) in contemporary Mexican and Argentine fiction. Sarah contributed to this project by compiling and categorizing primary and secondary sources on four 17th-century Spanish female authors: María de Zayas y Sotomayor,  Leonor de Meneses, Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra, and Ana Francisca Abarca de Bolea y Mur. Another part of Sarah’s work included annotating Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra’s Navidades de Madrid y noches entretenidas, identifying key themes and critical bibliography.

Email: revillas@student.ubc.ca
Website: https://sarahrevilla.github.io/

Patricio Robles is a Ph.D. candidate in Hispanic Studies at UBC. His dissertation examines texts about nature in the Southern Cone (Patagonia and Pampas). Focusing on the intersection of Affective theory and Ecocritical analysis, Patricio investigates how nature and culture have been perceived in post-Independence travel writing, gauchesca literature, and contemporary novels. He studied Anthropology at the University of Buenos Aires and Environmental Studies at FLACSO (Latin American Social Sciences Institute).

Patricio has been responsible for the website's design and for incorporating external visualization tools to enhance the user experience. He has also been our primary liaison with the digital humanities staff at UBC, ensuring that we stay up-to-date on the latest developments.

In addition to his work on the website, Patricio has been involved in compiling our annotated bibliography of Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra.
