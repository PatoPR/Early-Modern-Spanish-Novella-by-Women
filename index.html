ABOUT MODERNAS

Welcome to our website dedicated to exploring and celebrating the rich literary heritage of Spanish women writers from early modernity. Our mission is to bring to light the often-overlooked contributions of these remarkable women, including María de Zayas y Sotomayor, María Egual, Leonor de Meneses Noronha, Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra, and Ana Francisca Abarca de Bolea y Mur. These writers defied the gender roles of their time and broke through the barriers that held them back, leaving behind a legacy of powerful, insightful, and influential literary works that continue to inspire and captivate readers today. On this website, you’ll find in-depth research, insightful analysis, and fascinating historical context about the lives and works of these trailblazing writers. Our team of dedicated researchers is committed to uncovering the hidden gems of Spanish women’s literature, and we invite you to join us on this journey of discovery. We hope this website serves as a valuable resource for scholars, students, and enthusiasts alike and sparks a renewed appreciation for the groundbreaking contributions of Spanish women writers throughout history. Thank you for visiting, and we invite you to explore the fascinating world of early modern Spanish women’s literature with us. Key features of our website

Catalogue

Our catalogue provides a comprehensive resource for those interested in the female contributors to the Spanish novela corta of the 17th century. Each author is represented by an annotated bibliography, highlighting their most important works, as well as key themes that emerge from their writing. Primary sources, such as original manuscripts or early printed editions, are also included where available, alongside secondary sources that provide additional context and analysis. By compiling these materials in one place, our catalogue aims to facilitate a more nuanced understanding of the contributions made by María de Zayas y Sotomayor, Leonor de Meneses, Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra, María Egual, and Ana Francisca Abarca de Bolea y Mur to the literary landscape of Early Modern Spain..

Visualization Tools

Experience a unique and interactive exploration of our texts on Women Spanish writers from early modernity through our Visualization Tools page. Discover a fresh perspective on their literary works and authors by harnessing the power of cutting-edge tools like Voyant Tools. With Voyant Tools, we delve into distant reading, analyzing vast collections of texts collectively. Visualizing patterns, trends, and connections within the corpus of Women Spanish writers uncovers hidden insights and deepens our understanding of their contributions. Our visualization tools present texts and authors in an attractive and engaging manner. Interactive visualizations, word clouds, frequency charts, and more allow you to delve into the thematic richness, linguistic features, and narrative styles employed by these remarkable women writers..

Maps

Welcome to our Maps section, where we explore the geographical landscapes associated with Women Spanish writers from early modernity. Through interactive maps, we delve into the places where these authors lived, worked, and found inspiration for their masterpieces. Visualizing the locations mentioned in their novels and the real-world settings that influenced them, we provide a deeper understanding of the connection between their creations and the physical spaces they inhabited. Our interactive maps let you navigate regions, cities, and towns, uncovering the settings that shaped the narratives and themes explored by these talented women. Immerse yourself in their world as you explore their homes, the vividly described landscapes, and the cultural contexts that influenced their works..

Bibliography

Our team at Modernas has conducted extensive research and analysis on our writers. We have meticulously examined academic papers and articles dedicated to their works and produced a comprehensive and rigorously annotated bibliography. Recognizing the often overlooked contributions of women writers during this period, we have diligently curated a collection of scholarly resources that sheds light on their remarkable literary achievements. Our annotated bibliography covers various aspects, including thematic explorations, narrative techniques, and cultural contexts. Each entry within our bibliography has been carefully evaluated to ensure its scholarly significance and relevance to the field. By documenting and categorizing these resources, we have created a valuable reference tool for scholars, researchers, and enthusiasts interested in exploring the rich tapestry of early modern Spanish literature..

OUR TEAM

John Doe

Director

Elizabeth S. Lagresa-González obtained her Ph.D. in Romance Languages and Literatures from Harvard University and is an Assistant Professor at the University of British Columbia, Vancouver. She specializes in early modern Spanish literature and culture, which she addresses at the intersection of gender, cross-cultural, visual and material studies. She has published multiple peer-reviewed articles, a critical edition and English translation of Bernat Metge’s Lo Somni / The Dream, as well as a forthcoming co-edited volume on “Literature and Economy in Spain and Beyond” from the Revista Canadiense de Estudios Hispánicos. Her monograph, titled “The Business of Romance,” examines the transculturation of objects and female subjects across national and disciplinary borders through the lens of new economic and material criticism..

María Fernanda Guzmán

Research Assitant

María Fernanda Guzmán was born in Orizaba, Veracruz, Mexico, and has loved to write since she was little. She studied literature at the Universidad de las Américas Puebla and had two academic exchanges at City Beijing University (China) and the University of Salamanca (Spain). She just finished her M.A. in Hispanic Studies at the University of British Columbia. She has worked as an editor of books published by the Management of the Historic Center of Puebla. She was also a high school teacher at Prepa Anahuac Puebla. Her tastes are diverse: she likes to stream video games, read historical novels and write fantasy. She recently published her first book Arcángel: los niños elegidos..

Jennifer Nagtegaal

Research Assistant

Jennifer Nagtegaal is a Ph.D. candidate in Hispanic Studies at UBC. Jennifer’s M.A. and doctoral research, funded by the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada (SSHRC), develops in the areas of animation studies and comics studies, respectively, and a number of recent projects at the intersection of both. This is the case of her monograph, Politically Animated: Non-Fiction Animation from the Hispanic World, forthcoming with the University of Toronto Press (August 2023). Politically Animated offers the first book-length study on animated non-fiction from the Spanish-speaking world. Jennifer worked as a Graduate Research Assistant on this project from 2022-3. Jennifer’s early contributions focused on preparing a comprehensive bibliography for the women writers under study (specifically, primary and secondary sources for Leonor de Meneses and Ana Francisca Abarca de Bolea), as well as the annotation of these two individual bibliographies. A second phase of her work saw the translation of these annotated bibliographies into English..

Mirella Reichenbach Livotti

Research Assistant

Mirella Reichenbach Livotti is a Master´s student in the Hispanic Studies Program at the University of British Columbia (UBC). She also completed her undergraduate studies with a Double Major in Sociology and Latin American Studies from the same university. Her research interest is centred primarily on the intersection of gender and race in contemporary Latin American literature. In addition, she is interested in the representations of gender-based violence and its connection to work being done by feminist social movements and intellectuals. For this project, Re-imagining the Hispanic Novella, Mirella is responsible for creating a critical annotated bibliography of the author María Egual and her novel El esclavo de su dama. .

Sarah Revilla

Research Assistant

Sarah Revilla is a Ph.D. student in Hispanic Studies at UBC. Her research lies at the intersection of gender and cultural studies, particularly interested in gendered violence, affect, and artivism (art+activism) in contemporary Mexican and Argentine fiction. Sarah contributed to this project by compiling and categorizing primary and secondary sources on four 17th-century Spanish female authors: María de Zayas y Sotomayor, Leonor de Meneses, Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra, and Ana Francisca Abarca de Bolea y Mur. Another part of Sarah’s work included annotating Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra’s Navidades de Madrid y noches entretenidas, identifying key themes and critical bibliography.

Patricio Robles

Research Assistant. Web Designer

Patricio Robles is a Ph.D. candidate in Hispanic Studies at UBC. His dissertation examines texts about nature in the Southern Cone (Patagonia and Pampas). Focusing on the intersection of Affective theory and Ecocritical analysis, Patricio investigates how nature and culture have been perceived in post-Independence travel writing, gauchesca literature, and contemporary novels. He studied Anthropology at the University of Buenos Aires and Environmental Studies at FLACSO (Latin American Social Sciences Institute). Patricio has been responsible for the website’s design and for incorporating external visualization tools to enhance the user experience. He has also been our primary liaison with the digital humanities staff at UBC, ensuring that we stay up-to-date on the latest developments. In addition to his work on the website, Patricio has been involved in compiling our annotated bibliography of Mariana de Carvajal y Saavedra..

Pamela Zamora

Research Assistant. Web Designer

Pamela Zamora is a doctoral candidate in Hispanic Studies at the University of British Columbia. Her dissertation revolves around how 20th and 21st Central American and Mexican fiction contests humanitarian crises such feminicido, indigenous genocide and forced migration in the neoliberal epoch. At the intersection of sound studies, feminist theory and migration studies, her work amines to trace the literary representation of those crises. Pamela collected primary and critical sources to develop an exhaustive bibliography database on female writers from the Golden Age..
